üé§ AI Voice Cloning Scams

What It Is: Scammers can now copy someone's voice using just a few seconds of audio from social media, voicemails, or videos. They use this fake voice to call you pretending to be someone you know.
üìû Real-Life Scenario: The Grandparent Scam 2.0

What Happens:

You receive a call. It sounds EXACTLY like your grandson. He's crying and says: "Grandma, I've been in a car accident. I'm so scared. The police are here. I need $5,000 for bail right now. Please don't tell Mom and Dad, I'm so embarrassed. Can you wire the money?"

The voice sounds perfect. The emotion seems real. But it's a scam.

üö© Warning Signs:

  • Urgent requests for money, especially through wire transfer, gift cards, or cryptocurrency
  • Asking you to keep it secret from other family members
  • Pressure to act immediately without time to think
  • Unusual payment methods (gift cards, wire transfers, cash apps)
  • They won't let you call them back on their regular number

‚úÖ How to Protect Yourself:

  • Create a Family Code Word: Agree on a secret word or phrase with family members that only you know. Ask for it if someone calls asking for money.
  • Hang Up and Call Back: Always hang up and call the person directly using a number you already have saved.
  • Verify Through Another Family Member: Call another relative to check if the emergency is real.
  • Never Rush: Real emergencies can wait 10 minutes for you to verify. Scammers will pressure you to act NOW.
  • Ask Specific Questions: Ask something only the real person would know (pet's name, last place you visited together).

üé• Deepfake Video Scams

What It Is: AI can now create videos of people saying or doing things they never did. These "deepfakes" look incredibly realistic and are being used to trick people.
üì± Real-Life Scenario: The CEO Video Call

You receive a video call that appears to be from your bank manager or a company CEO. They look and sound exactly right. They say there's been suspicious activity on your account and they need you to verify your information immediately by providing your account number and password.

The video is fake, created by AI. Your real bank would NEVER ask for passwords.

Common Deepfake Scams:

  • Fake Celebrity Endorsements: Videos of celebrities promoting investments or products they've never heard of
  • Impersonation Scams: Someone pretending to be a government official, police officer, or company executive
  • Romance Scams: Creating fake video calls to build trust before asking for money
  • Investment Scams: Fake financial experts giving "insider tips"

üö© Signs It Might Be a Deepfake:

  • Slight blurriness or odd lighting around the face
  • Unnatural eye movements or blinking
  • Audio doesn't quite match lip movements
  • Jerky or glitchy movements when they turn their head
  • Unusual shadows or reflections
  • Background looks slightly off or blurred

‚úÖ How to Protect Yourself:

  • Be Skeptical of Video Calls from Unknown Numbers: Just because you can see someone doesn't mean they're real.
  • Verify Independently: Hang up and contact the person or organization through official channels.
  • Never Share Sensitive Information on Video Calls: No legitimate organization will ask for passwords, PINs, or full account numbers.
  • Watch for Pressure Tactics: Scammers create urgency. Real businesses give you time to verify.
  • Check Official Sources: If a celebrity is endorsing something, check their verified social media accounts.

Exercise 1: The Video Call from Your Bank

The Situation:

You receive a video call on your phone from someone who says they're from your bank's fraud department. You can see them clearly - they're wearing a suit and sitting in what looks like an office. They show you an ID badge. They say there's been suspicious activity on your account and they need to verify your identity by having you read out your account number and the 3-digit code on the back of your card.

üîç Look for these clues:
  • What are they asking for?
  • How did they contact you?
  • What's the urgency level?
‚úÖ CORRECT! This is a DEEPFAKE SCAM

Why: Your real bank will NEVER call you and ask for your full account number, card number, or security code - even on a video call. The professional appearance, office setting, and ID badge can all be faked or created with AI. The video of the "banker" could be a deepfake, or it could be a real person pretending to be from your bank. Either way, it's a scam!

What to do: Hang up immediately and call your bank using the number on the back of your card or from their official website.

Exercise 2: Celebrity Investment Video

The Situation:

You see a video on Facebook showing Tom Hanks talking about an "amazing investment opportunity" in cryptocurrency. He says he's made millions and you can too. The video looks professional, and it really looks and sounds like him. At the end, there's a link to "invest now" with a limited-time offer. You've never heard Tom Hanks talk about cryptocurrency before.

üîç Look for these clues:
  • Is this typical content for this celebrity?
  • Where did you find this video?
  • Is there a financial ask?
‚úÖ CORRECT! This is a DEEPFAKE SCAM

Why: Celebrity investment deepfakes are extremely common! Scammers use AI to create fake videos of famous people like Tom Hanks, Elon Musk, or Morgan Freeman promoting fake investments. The video quality might be good, but if a celebrity is promoting an investment opportunity you've never heard about through social media (not their verified accounts), it's almost certainly fake.

Red flags: Random social media post (not from verified account), financial opportunity, urgency ("limited time"), and content that's unusual for that celebrity.

What to do: Do NOT click the link. Check the celebrity's official verified social media accounts. Report the fake video to Facebook.

Exercise 3: Voice Call from Your Grandson

The Situation:

You get a phone call (voice only, no video) from your grandson's number. It sounds exactly like him. He says "Hey Grandma, my car broke down on the highway. I'm stuck and my wallet was in the car when it got towed. Can you send me $200 through Venmo so I can get an Uber home? I'm really stressed out." You talked to him just yesterday and everything seemed fine.

üîç Look for these clues:
  • What's being requested?
  • Can you verify it's really him?
  • Does the situation make sense?
ü§î This could be REAL or a SCAM - You need to VERIFY!

Why this is tricky: It's calling from his actual number (which can be faked/"spoofed"), it sounds like him (AI can clone voices), and it's a plausible emergency. BUT it could also be your real grandson in trouble!

What to do:

  • Say: "I'll call you right back in 2 minutes" and hang up
  • Call him back on the same number - if it's a scammer, they won't answer
  • Ask your family code word if you have one
  • Ask a question only he would know: "What did we have for dinner last time you visited?"
  • Call another family member to verify

Never send money without verification, even if it sounds real and urgent. A real emergency can wait 5 minutes for you to verify!

Exercise 4: Video Message from Your Doctor's Office

The Situation:

You receive an email from what appears to be your doctor's office email address. It contains a video message from your doctor reminding you about your upcoming appointment next Tuesday at 2 PM. The doctor looks normal, mentions the appointment you actually have scheduled, and reminds you to bring your insurance card. There's no request for money or personal information.

üîç Look for these clues:
  • Is there a financial request?
  • Does the information match your records?
  • Is this typical for your doctor's office?
‚úÖ This is LIKELY REAL - but always verify!

Why this seems legitimate: Many doctor's offices are now using video appointment reminders. This message has accurate information (your real appointment), doesn't ask for money or sensitive information, and comes from what appears to be the office's email.

However, stay cautious: If you want to be absolutely sure, call your doctor's office using the number you have saved (not one from the email) and ask if they sent it. Email addresses can be spoofed to look real.

Key point: Not every video is a deepfake! Legitimate organizations do use video messages. The difference is they won't ask for passwords, payments, or urgent action.

Exercise 5: Video Call About Computer Virus

The Situation:

A pop-up appears on your computer screen, and then you receive a video call on your computer. The person on the video is wearing a Microsoft uniform and says they've detected a serious virus on your computer. They say they need to remote access your computer to fix it immediately. They look professional and the video quality is good, though you notice their face looks slightly blurry around the edges and their blinking seems odd.

üîç Look for these clues:
  • How did this call start?
  • What are they asking for?
  • Are there visual oddities?
‚úÖ CORRECT! This is a DEEPFAKE SCAM

Why: This is a classic tech support scam now using deepfake technology! Microsoft, Apple, and other tech companies NEVER call you unsolicited, and they NEVER use pop-ups to contact you about viruses. The blurry edges and odd blinking are common signs of deepfake video.

Major red flags:

  • Unsolicited contact about computer problems
  • Pop-up that led to a call (this is automated scam software)
  • Request for remote access to your computer
  • Visual anomalies (blurry face, odd blinking)

What to do: Close everything immediately. Do NOT give them access to your computer. Restart your computer if needed. Run your own legitimate antivirus if you have one installed.

Exercise 6: Voice Call from the Police

The Situation:

You receive a phone call from someone who says they're Officer Johnson from your local police department. They sound professional and official. They say there's a warrant for your arrest for missing jury duty, but you can clear it up right now by paying a $500 fine over the phone with a credit card. They say if you don't pay immediately, officers will come to arrest you within the hour. They sound very convincing and you're scared.

üîç Look for these clues:
  • Do police handle things this way?
  • What are the pressure tactics?
  • What payment method is requested?
‚úÖ CORRECT! This is an AI VOICE SCAM (possibly using voice synthesis)

Why: This is 100% a scam! Real police and courts do NOT:

  • Call you about warrants
  • Demand immediate payment over the phone
  • Accept credit cards for fines
  • Threaten immediate arrest if you don't pay right now
  • Handle legal matters entirely by phone

The reality: If you missed jury duty or had a warrant, you would receive official written notices in the mail first. You would have time to respond and handle it properly through the courts.

What to do: Hang up immediately. If you're worried it might be real, call your local police department using a number you look up yourself (not a number they give you) and ask them directly.

üéØ Key Takeaways from These Exercises:

  • Requests for money, passwords, or remote access = RED FLAG
  • Pressure and urgency = Scammer tactic
  • Always verify through independent channels
  • Watch for visual oddities in videos (blurriness, odd movements)
  • Just because it looks or sounds real doesn't mean it is
  • When in doubt, hang up and verify independently

üìß Modern Email & Text Scams

üì® Real-Life Scenario: The Amazon Alert

You get a text: "Amazon Alert: Unusual activity detected. Someone tried to purchase a $799 iPhone using your account. If this wasn't you, click here immediately to cancel: amazon-security-check.com"

It looks official, but it's a scam. The link leads to a fake website that steals your information.

Common Email/Text Scams:

  • Package Delivery Scams: "Your package can't be delivered, click here to reschedule"
  • Account Verification: "Your account will be closed unless you verify now"
  • Prize Notifications: "You've won! Claim your prize by clicking here"
  • IRS/Tax Scams: "You owe back taxes, pay immediately to avoid arrest"
  • Tech Support Scams: "Your computer has a virus, call this number"

üö© Red Flags in Messages:

  • Urgent language: "Act now!" "Immediate action required!" "Account will be closed!"
  • Threats of consequences: arrest, account closure, missed delivery
  • Requests to click links or download attachments
  • Asking for personal information via email or text
  • Spelling or grammar mistakes
  • Sender email doesn't match the company (amazonn.com instead of amazon.com)
  • Generic greetings like "Dear Customer" instead of your name

‚úÖ Safe Practices:

  • Never Click Links in Unexpected Messages: Go directly to the company's website by typing it yourself.
  • Hover Over Links (on computer): Before clicking, hover your mouse to see where it really goes.
  • Contact Companies Directly: Use phone numbers from their official website, not from the email or text.
  • Don't Trust Caller ID: Numbers can be faked. Always verify independently.
  • Delete Suspicious Messages: When in doubt, delete it.
Remember: The IRS, Social Security Administration, and your bank will NEVER call, text, or email asking for passwords, PINs, or full account numbers. They will NEVER threaten immediate arrest or demand payment via gift cards.

üîê Password Safety & Account Security

Strong passwords are your first line of defense. Weak passwords are like leaving your front door unlocked with a "Welcome!" sign.

‚ùå Common Password Mistakes:

  • Using the same password for multiple accounts
  • Simple passwords like "password123" or "qwerty"
  • Using birthdays, pet names, or other easy-to-guess information
  • Writing passwords on sticky notes near your computer
  • Sharing passwords with others

‚úÖ Creating Strong Passwords:

  • Use Passphrases: Instead of "Password1", try "BlueCoffee!Morning@2024" - easier to remember, harder to crack
  • Make Them Long: At least 12-16 characters
  • Mix It Up: Combine uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols
  • Unique for Each Account: Never reuse passwords, especially for banking or email
  • Consider a Password Manager: Apps like LastPass or 1Password safely store your passwords

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA):

What It Is: A second security check after your password. Even if someone steals your password, they can't get into your account without the second factor.

Example: You enter your password, then you receive a text message with a 6-digit code that you also need to enter. Or you might use an app on your phone that generates codes.

Enable 2FA on:
  • Email accounts (Gmail, Yahoo, etc.)
  • Banking and financial accounts
  • Social media accounts
  • Shopping accounts (Amazon, etc.)
  • Any account with sensitive information

What to Do If You're Hacked:

  1. Change your password immediately on the affected account
  2. Change passwords on other accounts if you used the same password
  3. Enable two-factor authentication
  4. Check for unauthorized transactions if it's a financial account
  5. Alert your bank if financial information was compromised
  6. Report it to the company and consider reporting to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov
